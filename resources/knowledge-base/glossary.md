# AI Governance Glossary

A comprehensive glossary of AI governance terminology, concepts, and definitions.

## A

**Accountability**
The principle that individuals, organizations, and AI systems should be responsible for their actions and decisions. In AI governance, accountability involves establishing clear lines of responsibility for AI system development, deployment, and outcomes.

**Adversarial Example**
An input specifically designed to cause an AI model to make a mistake or produce incorrect output. Adversarial examples are used to test model robustness and identify vulnerabilities.

**AI Ethics**
The branch of ethics that examines moral issues raised by artificial intelligence, including fairness, transparency, accountability, privacy, and the societal impact of AI systems.

**AI Lifecycle**
The complete journey of an AI system from conception to retirement, including data collection, model development, deployment, monitoring, maintenance, and decommissioning.

**AI Risk Management Framework (RMF)**
A structured approach to identifying, assessing, and mitigating risks associated with AI systems. The NIST AI RMF is a prominent example.

**Algorithmic Bias**
Systematic and repeatable errors in an AI system that create unfair outcomes, such as privileging one group over another. Can arise from biased training data, model design, or deployment context.

**Algorithmic Fairness**
The principle that AI systems should make decisions without unfair discrimination or prejudice. Various mathematical definitions exist, including demographic parity, equalized odds, and individual fairness.

**Audit Trail**
A chronological record of system activities, decisions, and changes that enables reconstruction and examination of events. Essential for AI governance and compliance.

## B

**Bias Mitigation**
Techniques and methods used to reduce or eliminate bias in AI systems. Can occur during data collection, model training (in-processing), or after deployment (post-processing).

**Black Box Model**
An AI model whose internal workings are not easily understood or interpreted by humans, making it difficult to explain how inputs are transformed into outputs.

## C

**Compliance**
Adherence to laws, regulations, standards, and internal policies governing AI development and deployment. Includes GDPR, CCPA, and industry-specific regulations.

**Concept Drift**
The phenomenon where the statistical properties of the target variable (what the model is predicting) change over time, potentially degrading model performance.

**Conformity Assessment**
The process of demonstrating that an AI system meets specified requirements, often required for regulatory compliance.

## D

**Data Governance**
The management of data availability, usability, integrity, and security in an organization. Critical for AI systems that depend on high-quality data.

**Data Lineage**
The documentation of data's origins, movements, transformations, and dependencies throughout its lifecycle. Essential for understanding and validating AI systems.

**Data Sheet**
A document that provides information about a dataset, including its motivation, composition, collection process, recommended uses, and limitations.

**Differential Privacy**
A mathematical framework for measuring and limiting privacy loss when analyzing datasets, ensuring individual privacy while allowing aggregate insights.

**Disparate Impact**
Discrimination that results from policies or practices that appear neutral but have a disproportionately adverse effect on protected groups.

## E

**Equalized Odds**
A fairness criterion requiring that a model's true positive rate and false positive rate are equal across different groups.

**Ethical AI**
AI systems designed and deployed in accordance with ethical principles, considering impacts on individuals, society, and the environment.

**Explainability**
The ability to explain or present an AI system's decision-making process in understandable terms to humans. See also Interpretability.

## F

**Fairness**
The quality of being free from bias or injustice in AI systems. Multiple mathematical definitions exist, and fairness criteria can sometimes conflict.

**Feature Importance**
A measure of how much each input feature contributes to a model's predictions, useful for model interpretation and debugging.

**Foundation Model**
Large-scale AI models trained on broad data that can be adapted to various downstream tasks. Examples include GPT-4, Claude, and BERT.

## G

**GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)**
European Union regulation on data protection and privacy, with implications for AI systems that process personal data.

**Governance Framework**
A structured set of policies, processes, and controls for managing AI systems throughout their lifecycle.

**Guardrails**
Constraints or safeguards built into AI systems to prevent undesired behaviors or outputs.

## H

**Human-in-the-Loop**
An AI system design where human judgment is incorporated into the decision-making process, particularly for critical or ambiguous cases.

**Human Rights Impact Assessment**
An evaluation of how an AI system may affect human rights, used to identify and mitigate potential harms.

## I

**Impact Assessment**
A systematic evaluation of the potential effects of an AI system on individuals, groups, or society.

**Individual Fairness**
A fairness criterion requiring that similar individuals receive similar treatment from an AI system.

**Interpretability**
The degree to which a human can understand the cause of a decision made by an AI system. Related to but distinct from explainability.

## L

**Lifecycle Management**
The systematic approach to managing an AI system through all phases of its existence, from development to retirement.

## M

**Model Card**
A standardized document providing information about a machine learning model, including its intended use, performance characteristics, and limitations.

**Model Governance**
The policies, processes, and controls for managing machine learning models throughout their lifecycle.

**Model Monitoring**
Continuous tracking of model performance, behavior, and impact in production to detect issues like drift or bias.

## P

**Predictive Parity**
A fairness criterion requiring that positive predictive value (precision) is equal across different groups.

**Privacy-Preserving ML**
Machine learning techniques that protect individual privacy, including differential privacy, federated learning, and secure multi-party computation.

## R

**Red Teaming**
The practice of deliberately testing an AI system to find vulnerabilities, biases, or potential harms before deployment.

**Responsible AI**
The practice of developing and deploying AI systems in ways that are ethical, fair, transparent, and accountable.

**Risk Assessment**
The systematic process of identifying, analyzing, and evaluating risks associated with an AI system.

**Robustness**
The ability of an AI system to maintain performance under various conditions, including adversarial attacks and distribution shifts.

## S

**Statistical Parity**
A fairness criterion requiring that a model's positive prediction rate is equal across different groups. Also called demographic parity.

**Supervised Learning**
A machine learning approach where models are trained on labeled data to make predictions on new, unlabeled data.

## T

**Transparency**
The principle that AI systems and their decision-making processes should be open and understandable to relevant stakeholders.

**Trustworthy AI**
AI systems that are reliable, safe, fair, transparent, accountable, and respect privacy and human rights.

## V

**Validation**
The process of evaluating an AI model's performance on a dataset separate from the training data to assess generalization.

**Value Alignment**
Ensuring that an AI system's objectives and behaviors align with human values and societal norms.
